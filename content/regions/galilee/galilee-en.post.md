## TITLE
High Ground: Wine Country in the Galilee


## BODY

Israel's most mountainous wine country, and the place that proved the north is not just a pretty region, but a quality one.

**At a Glance**
Where: the far north, Israel's mountainous wine country with elevations ranging mostly from 500-900m with mountains reaching as high as 1200m above sea level.
Climate: the coolest and wettest of any Israeli wine region with parts of it reaching an average of  800 - 1,000mm of rain a year, a significantly higher average than  almost anywhere else in the country.
Soil: a patchwork that changes slope to slope - terra rossa, volcanic basalt, and limestone.


**The Story**

Grapevines have grown in these hills since biblical times - this was Naphtali's territory. Many ancient wine presses were found all around the Galilee including a truly unique ancient wine press with Talmudic period mosaic floor. But the Galilee everyone talks about today is a much younger story, and it starts with a simple discovery.

Israel is mostly a hot, dry country. The Galilee's advantage is that it isn't, not up high. Growers who planted in the Upper Galilee East from the mid- 1990s onward found something rare here - real altitude, real rainfall, real cool nights - the closest thing Israel has to a classic, european  temperate wine climate. It changed what people believed Israeli wine could be. Where else in the country do you get 800–1,000mm of rain in a single year? The Galilee doesn't need to fight its climate the way most of Israel's wine regions do. It works with it. Since then the Israeli world of wine has come a long way in understanding how to work with each region, but these were early days.

The name itself has been on a label since 1979, when Carmel's Special Reserve became the first Israeli wine to mention "Galilee" on the back. But the region as we know it today really starts in 1995, when Dalton Winery planted here and became the modern pioneer - the first of the names that built the Galilee's reputation.

The soil adds to the puzzle in the best way. Move a few hundred metres along a Galilee hillside and you can cross from terra rossa to volcanic basalt to loose limestone gravel . Add in Upper Galilee West's own hilly microclimates and the gentler Lower Galilee below, and you get a region that behaves less like one place and more like a mosaic of small, distinct neighborhoods, each pulling a little differently on the grapes grown there.

Vineyards are everywhere in the Galilee, this is truly a beautiful wine growing region - terraced hillsides, cool mist in the mornings, vines running right up into pine forest. Wineries like Dalton, Or Haganuz, Lahat, Lueria, and Galil Mountain built their names here, and between them they cover a wide stretch of what the Galilee can do.


**Why the Altitude Matters**

Altitude sounds like a minor detail, but in the Galilee it's the whole story.

Go up in elevation and the air gets cooler - simple enough, but the effect on wine is significant. Grapes ripen more gradually with cool temperatures at height, which gives them time to build flavor without racing straight to sugar. The vine isn't rushed, and neither is the fruit.

Rain matters just as much, and the Galilee gets more of it than the rest of the country by a wide margin. That means less dependence on irrigation, and vines that develop a steadier, more natural rhythm through the growing season. It also means cooler nights than a hot climate region sees, which helps hold onto the fresh acidity that gives a wine its lift and ageing potential.

Then there's the soil variety, which sounds like it should make things harder to manage - and in a sense it does - but it's also why no single grape or style dominates here the way it might in a more uniform region. Terra rossa, volcanic basalt, and limestone each pull differently on a vine's roots, so a Galilee winery with plots across a few of these soil types has real range to work with, plot by plot.

Put together - altitude, rain, cool nights, varied soil - and you get the one thing a hot country rarely can: freshness that comes naturally, not from a winemaking trick to compensate for it.


**What to Expect in the Glass**

The Galilee isn't a one- style region, and that's really the point of it. Between Upper Galilee East's cooler heights, Upper Western Galilee's own microclimates, and the gentler Lower Galilee, you'll find serious, structured reds built for aging alongside brighter, more aromatic whites - often from the same producer, sometimes from vineyards a short drive apart.

What ties it together is freshness. Wherever a Galilee wine comes from, it tends to carry real acidity and real lift. Beyond that throughline, the honest approach is the same one that works for any diverse region: taste across a few producers and let the differences show you what the Galilee can do, rather than expecting one definitive "Galilee style."


**Wineries to Visit**

- Dalton - Upper Galilee, one of the region's most established names.
- Or Haganuz - Upper Galilee, boutique.
- Lueria - Upper Galilee.
- Galil Mountain - a good introduction to the region's range.
- Adir winery, Dalton and Recanati wineries are all walking distance from each other
