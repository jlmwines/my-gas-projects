## TITLE
Highland Spine: Wine Country in the Central Mountains


## BODY

*The mountain spine that runs the length of the country — and the Jerusalem corridor that became Israel's proof of quality wine.*


**At a Glance**
Where: the highland spine running north to south through the country's middle — from Mt. Gilboa in the north, through the Shomron hills, down through the Jerusalem corridor of the Judean Hills (from the hills north of the city to Gush Etzion in the south).
Climate: warm days, cool nights at elevation — 400 to 1,000 meters in the Judean Hills; snow reaches the higher peaks some winters.
Soil: thin, stony terra rossa laid over deep limestone bedrock.


**The Story**

This is Israel's mountain spine — one continuous highland running the length of the country, from the Gilboa in the north down through the Shomron hills to the ridge that carries Jerusalem itself. But the story people actually tell about this ground starts in one specific stretch: the Judean Hills, the corridor of high ground running north and south of the city.

For most of Israeli wine's modern history, this ground barely factored into it. That changed in 1992, when Domaine du Castel planted here and released the first wine to put the region on a label at all — Haut Judée, in the French they chose for it. Tzora Vineyards followed a year later, in 1993. Between them, they turned a stretch of hillside outside Jerusalem into one of the two or three most talked-about addresses in Israeli wine, and the plantings that followed through the 2000s only built on what they'd started.

Some of the older plots here are still worked the traditional way — vines planted on stone-lined terraces climbing the slope, roots that have spent decades digging down through the stony ground for water, no irrigation needed at all. It's some of the most physically demanding wine country in Israel to farm, and also some of the most rewarding.

Head north from Jerusalem along the same spine and the story keeps going, just more quietly. The Shomron hills carry their own newer generation of vineyards, planted mostly from the 2000s onward at similar elevation, still building the reputation the Judean Hills already has. Keep going north and the spine reaches Mt. Gilboa, its northern shoulder, where the highland finally starts to give way to the valley below.

It's a landscape with old names attached to it, too — this was Ephraim and the western half of Manasseh's territory up in the Shomron, with Benjamin and Judah's around Jerusalem itself. People, and vines, have used these mountains a long time before this generation's wineries got here.


**A Spine With Two Faces**

The single strangest thing about this region is how fast it changes character. Stand at 1,000 meters in the Judean Hills and it's genuinely alpine — cool mountain air, stone terraces, snow some winters. Head a short drive east and the same spine falls away into the Judean Desert, dry and bare, on its way down to the Dead Sea, the lowest point on Earth. No other Israeli wine region moves between two such different worlds in so short a distance.

That drop is exactly what makes the Judean Hills' vineyards work the way they do. The altitude buys the cool nights; the desert just over the ridge is a reminder of how little margin for error the vines actually have. It's a highland that has to hold onto every advantage it gets.

It's tempting to reach for a European comparison here — a cool, terraced hillside near a historic city invites it. But it's worth being honest about the difference: in a place like Burgundy or the Rhône, centuries of tradition and appellation law do a lot of the work of shaping what a wine tastes like. In the Judean Hills, that inherited structure doesn't exist yet. What shapes the wine here is mostly the altitude, the stony ground, and the choices an individual winemaker makes with them — terroir is real, but it shares the credit rather than owning it outright.


**What to Expect in the Glass**

The Judean Hills built their name on structured reds with real backbone — the kind that reward a few years in the cellar — alongside crisp, cool-climate whites from the same high vineyards. The common thread, wherever in the spine the grapes come from, is that cooler-night freshness: acidity that holds up, tannins that need time rather than force. It's a serious, patient region, more likely to reward a second glass than the first — not a wine to open the same day you buy it, but one worth setting aside for a few years and forgetting about.


**Wineries to Visit**

- Domaine du Castel — Judean Hills, the region's modern pioneer, planted 1992.
- Tzora Vineyards — Judean Hills, planted a year after Castel, in 1993.
- Gvaot — Shomron hills, part of the newer generation of plantings there.
- Tura — Shomron hills, another of the region's newer names.
