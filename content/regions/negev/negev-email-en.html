The Desert Is Making Wine Again

Fifteen hundred years ago, the Negev was wine country. The Nabataeans terraced these hills, caught flash floods for irrigation, and shipped a wine the Romans called Vinum Gazentum — "Gaza Wine" — across Europe and the Near East. Then the desert went quiet for more than a thousand years.

"If you're stubborn enough, any place can be farmed" — that's the idea behind the modern revival. Drip irrigation, invented in Israel in the 1950s, made the impossible merely difficult, and by the late 1990s growers were planting vines again at Ramat Arad and Mitzpe Ramon. There's an even stranger twist: archaeologists recently found 1,500-year-old grape pips in the old Byzantine ruins, and two ancient varieties grown from them — Sariki and Be'er — are now being replanted at Avdat and Shivta.

I wanted you to hear the whole story — how the desert actually works in wine's favor, and which producers are worth trying today.
