## TITLE
Wine in the Desert: The Negev


## BODY

Israel's desert frontier, where wine is both the country's oldest story and its boldest modern initiative.

**At a Glance**
Where: the southern desert, more than half the country. Main vineyards around Mitzpe Ramon (about 800m up) and Ramat Arad.
Climate: scorching days, cold nights, very scarce rain.
Soil: pale desert loess (fine, wind-blown silt, with good water retention) over limestone.


**The Story**

Fifteen hundred years ago, the Negev was wine country. It is hard to picture now, looking at the bare hills, but the desert once shipped wine across the whole Mediterranean. In tribal times this was the southern frontier of Judah, with Shimon's towns out toward Beersheba — but the Negev's real story is the desert itself.

The Nabataeans, the traders who built Petra, prospered running the great Spice Route. This desert highway carried frankincense and myrrh from Arabia to the Mediterranean, and the towns they raised out of the sand — Avdat, Shivta, Nizzana, Mamshit — were its stations. Wine became a second cargo alongside the spices.

The Nabataeans, and the Byzantines after them, were brilliant at catching water: they terraced the slopes, channeled the rare flash floods into their vineyards, and even built towers to collect pigeon droppings for fertilizer. Out of all that effort came a sweet white wine the Romans called Vinum Gazentum, "Gaza Wine," named for the port it sailed from, where the Spice Route met the sea. It was a luxury export, consumed across Europe and the Near East. One town alone, Shivta, may have produced around two million litres yearly.

Due to economic, environmental, and demographic changes, sometime in the seventh century CE the vineyards were abandoned. The cities emptied, and the desert went quiet for more than a thousand years.

The modern chapter starts with a very Israeli idea: if you are stubborn enough, any place can be farmed. Drip irrigation, invented in Israel in the late 1950s, made the impossible merely difficult. In the late 90s growers began planting vines in the Negev again, at Ramat Arad and near Mitzpe Ramon, sometimes irrigating with salty water drawn from wells hundreds of metres underground. The results were unimpressive. In 1997 Alon Zadok of Ramat Negev winery planted the first quality grapes and made the first wines raised and produced in the Negev. "Making the desert bloom" became reality.

Another fascinating twist came recently. Archaeologists digging in the old Byzantine sites found grape pips that had survived in the dry soil for around 1,500 years. DNA testing identified two local varieties, Sariki and Be'er, possible parents of that famous Gazentum Wine. In 2023 a vineyard of these revived ancient grapes was planted at Avdat, with more following at Shivta. The desert is again producing these grapes and their wines, once thought long lost.


**Why Grow Grapes in the Desert?**

A desert sounds like a terrible place to grow wine, but the Negev has a few things working in its favor.

Start with the soil. The pale loess covering these hills is fine, wind-blown dust over limestone, reflective and low in fertility. Poor soil sounds like a problem, but vines that are challenged focus their energy on fewer, and more concentrated grapes. The limestone beneath drains well and holds just enough moisture to matter in a place with almost no rain.

Then the day-night temperature difference is the desert's real gift. Hot afternoons drive the vine's photosynthesis hard, building the sugars that will eventually become alcohol (though past a certain heat the vine partly closes down to protect itself). This process is further complemented by cold desert nights. When the temperature drops sharply after dark, the vine's metabolism slows down, and the acids it would otherwise burn through in the day's heat remain. This way, the fruit can ripen fully without losing crucial acidity. Natural sugars and freshness remain together, something usually destroyed by a hot climate.

The dryness helps too: with so little humidity, vine diseases and many vineyard pests struggle to take hold, so the grapes grow clean with very minimal pesticides.

The hard part is water, and always has been, from the Nabataeans' flood channels to today's drip lines, sometimes drawn salty from wells hundreds of metres down. This is the most striking contrast between the Negev and classic European wine regions. In many of the classical regions, no watering is allowed. Here, human ingenuity does at least as much as the soil. The Negev has no Old World counterpart to compare it to, and that is exactly the point.


**What to Expect in the Glass**

Unlike established and Old World regions, the Negev wine production is too young and too varied to have one desert style or characteristic. A variety of producers with different altitudes and attitudes making different choices about everything from irrigation to oak barrels adds up to a range of experience, rather than a distinct profile. The one thread they share is the conditions behind them: fruit ripened fully by the sun, often with a line of freshness from those cold nights. Beyond that, the best approach is to taste across producers rather than expect a type. The revived ancient grapes are newer still, only a few bottled so far, and far too early to generalise about at all.
