## TITLE
The Grapes of Israel: Familiar Names, Different Wines


## BODY

Say "Cabernet Sauvignon" or "Chardonnay" to most people and they think they already know what's coming. In Israel, that's only half true. The names are familiar. What's actually in the glass, and what's actually growing in the ground, often isn't what you'd expect.

**The Big Three**

Cabernet Sauvignon is Israel's single most-planted grape, covering about a fifth of the country's 5,500 hectares of vineyard. The reason isn't fashion, it's a real problem the grape has to solve here: Israel's heat builds sugar fast, and the hard part is holding onto enough acid to balance it. The fix has been to plant high — up in the Golan Heights, the Galilee, the Judean Hills, sometimes past 800 meters — where the air runs cooler and the swing between hot days and cold nights slows the grape down and protects its acid. Merlot, right behind Cabernet at around 16% of plantings, works the same way, just with softer tannin and a rounder feel. Both sometimes carry a real, distinctive note of mint or eucalyptus alongside the dark fruit — not a flaw, just what grows here.

Then there's Carignan, and this is where the story gets interesting. When Baron Edmond de Rothschild's agronomists planted Israel's first modern vineyards in 1882, Carignan was one of their first choices, picked simply because it grows easily in a Mediterranean climate. For the next hundred-plus years it was the grape nobody talked about and everybody drank — the anonymous backbone of Israeli wine, at one point covering something like 40% of everything planted in the entire country. Then the 1980s quality revolution came, chasing the reputations of Cabernet and Merlot, and Carignan's share was cut nearly in half. It looked done. Instead, winemakers went back to the oldest surviving vines — too low-yielding for bulk wine, but exactly the kind that make something serious — and old-vine Carignan is now one of the signature wines of the Coastal Plain, the very region where those first 1882 vines went into the ground. The grape that built the industry, got taken for granted, and then earned its way back.

**The Whites Nobody Talks About**

Here's a real surprise: Israel's two biggest white grapes by area aren't Chardonnay or Sauvignon Blanc. They're Colombard and Emerald Riesling, and most wine drinkers have never heard of either. That's because their real job isn't the wine glass — it's the brandy still. Colombard is the backbone of Israeli brandy production, prized for the same reason it's used in French Cognac and Armagnac: high acid and a flavor plain enough that it doesn't get in the way once it's distilled.

Emerald Riesling has an even better story. It's not a natural grape at all — it was built. American scientist Harold Olmo bred it at UC Davis specifically to survive hot climates without losing its acid, releasing it in 1948. Israel turned out to be one of the places it actually works best, usually made into an off-dry white worth seeking out on its own terms.

Meanwhile, Chardonnay and Sauvignon Blanc dominate the boutique conversation without dominating the vineyard. Chardonnay is flexible almost to a fault — its character comes mostly from the winemaker's choices, not the climate — and it's been considered Israel's finest white since the late 1980s. Sauvignon Blanc gets its freshness from deliberate choices too: planted higher up, picked earlier, fermented cold in steel tanks, all aimed at keeping it crisp and citrusy rather than heavy.

**The Interesting Ones**

A few grapes here have real, specific stories worth knowing. Malbec is one of them, and it comes with a confession: I used to reject Israeli Malbec, over and over, because I kept expecting Argentina's version — huge, dark, intense, the product of Mendoza's extreme high-altitude sun. Israel doesn't have Mendoza's altitude or that intensity of UV, so our Malbec never gets pushed into that same register. It took me a while to stop comparing and just taste what was actually in the glass. Once I did, I loved it. Funny enough, Malbec was also one of Rothschild's original 1880s imports, right alongside Carignan and Cabernet — it's been here the whole time.

Barbera tells a similar story from the other direction. In its native Piedmont, cooler weather keeps its naturally high acid sharp and tart. Israel's warmer days ripen it further before that acid backs off, so it comes out gentler and rounder here — more ripe cherry, less bite. Gewürztraminer needs the cold of the northern Golan specifically to develop its full aromatic character, usually finishing semi-dry. And Argaman is a genuinely Israeli invention: bred in 1972 at the Volcani Institute by crossing Souzão and Carignan, purpose-built to fix Carignan's one real weakness — pale color — and named "crimson" in Hebrew for the deep color it delivers.

**The Ancient Ones**

Israel's grape story doesn't only run forward. In the Judea/Bethlehem area, researchers have spent years reviving indigenous varieties — Marawi, Hamdani, Bittuni, Dabouki, Jandali — grapes with roots in the region's ancient winemaking past, now bottled commercially again. And in the Negev, archaeologists found grape pips in Byzantine ruins that had survived 1,500 years in the dry desert soil; DNA work identified two lost varieties, Sariki and Be'er, now replanted at Avdat and Shivta. Only a handful of bottles exist so far, but the desert is quite literally growing its own history back.

**No Rules About Any of This**

One more thing sets Israel apart: there's no appellation law telling winemakers what they're allowed to plant or blend. A winery can mix Cabernet with Carignan with Argaman with a Rhône grape, all in the same bottle, and nobody's stopping them. It's not a shortcut. It's freedom — and it's a big part of why Israeli wine keeps surprising people who thought they already knew what these grapes were supposed to taste like.
